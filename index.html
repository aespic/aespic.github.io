Commercial Real Estate and Credit Misallocation
This paper accounts for the heightened sensitivity of Commercial Real Estate (CRE) investors to credit conditions and examines its macroeconomic implications. I first document that CRE investors hold a large share of capital and face looser credit standards than other non-financial firms, owing to the higher pledgeability of their assets. I then incorporate CRE investors into a two-sector general equilibrium model with heterogeneous borrowing constraints. This feature generates sectoral misallocation: capital accumulated by CRE investors yields lower expected returns than capital held by other non-financial firms. I show that this misallocation is substantial, both in steady state and over the credit cycle, by calibrating the model to French macro and micro data. Because sectoral capital stocks are imperfect substitutes, policies that reallocate credit from CRE investors toward other non-financial firms raise welfare, particularly when implemented during the boom phase of a credit cycle.

Capital Requirements in Light of Monetary Tightening, with Lisa Kerdelhué and Julien Matheron
In 2021, euro area inflation surged, prompting the European Central Bank to reverse its monetary stance. This paper argues that this shift did not generate significant financial stress, partly because of the bank capital requirements introduced in the 2010s. We develop a framework to assess their effects over the business cycle. Although capital requirements remained broadly stable, they shaped the transmission of the structural shocks underlying this episode. We find that while these requirements modestly constrained post-Covid growth, they successfully prevented the materialization of risks. Overall, capital requirements strengthened the economy’s resilience to adverse shocks at a relatively low macroeconomic cost.
– Forthcoming in the International Journal of Central Banking, April 2026

Public Debt as Private Liquidity: The Poincaré Experience (1926–1929)
In the follow-up to the 1926 political and monetary crisis in France, a new government led by Raymond Poincaré attempted to restore monetary stability by restructuring public debt. A sinkinfund was missioned to withdraw short-term public bills from money markets. This policy disorganized the largest Parisian banks of the time, as they relied on these bills to manage their liquidity. Without developed domestic money markets, no other asset could absorb the excessliquidity freed by the withdrawal of these bills, and these leading banks faced a low-rate environment. In search of yield, they expanded their activities abroad a few months before the 1929 crash. These findings renew our understanding of the expansion of France's banking sector in the 1920s. In addition, they shed new light on the role of public debt in financial stability in an open economy.
Financial Histoiry Review, 2023, 30(3):308-329
